What makes a titletown?

The Winningest Cities in North American Sports

When Golden State won its third NBA championship in four years a few months ago, I started thinking. Of all the cities to field a professional or college level team in the last 150 years, which is the winningest?

Is it Green Bay, WI, a city literally called Titletown? Or maybe it’s some place bigger, like New York City.

Well, that depends. I've dug through the data and come up with three cases in which any of these cities could be crowned Titletown. Before we dive in though, we’ve got to lay out some ground rules. Use the filters in the bottom left to set your parameters and we'll get started.

Actual Titles

Our first way to determine titletowns is to simply look at the numbers — total championships won by a metro area. And in this case, looking at all sports between 1870 and 2018, the winningest city in North America is Greater Los Angeles, CA.
This makes sense, considering Greater Los Angeles teams have played a combined x seasons . But is 74 titles what we should expect based on the number of opportunites they’ve had to win?
Well actually, no. We’d expect them to have won a lot fewer. By giving each team in a given season an equal chance of winning its respective championship, and adding those odds up over time, we can get an expected number of championships. Combining these for each metro area to ever field a team, we can compare the expected to actual wins differential for every city in our dataset. Greater Los Angeles should have won 24.

Conversion Rates

But what about a metro area’s performance over time? How has the idea of a titletown changed over the past 150 years?
Let’s consider a metro area’s conversion rate — that is, how well a city’s teams perform when given a real shot at winning it all. Between 1870 and 1920, in all sports, the best teams came from New Haven (the Yale Bulldogs competed in 47 seasons, winning 17 times, or in 36% of their attempts) — Vancouver teams hadn’t even started playing yet.
Between 1920 and 1970, however, New Haven falls of the map, replaced by New York, whose teams won a title in 35 of 51 combined seasons (a 69% conversion rate!), and Toronto, which went 23 for 51 for a 45% conversion.
In the last 48 years though, it’s all Los Angeles. As a city, LA has won 50 championships since 1970 — a conversion rate of 104% — and has finished in the final four or better 131 times.

So where’s the real titletown here? The winningest city in North America has, of course, changed over time. Let’s look at metro areas that have been good (or bad) for a long time.

Dry Spells and Dynasties

If we look at streaks of final four appearances, we can see how long a city has been good for. And if we look at dynasties — championships in a row, — we can see how long a metro area has been great for. Maybe more importantly though, we can look at dry spells. Every city’s going to go some time without making the top four, but for me, a metro area that can avoid that as much as possible is a real titletown.

Home Field Advantage

There is also a relationship between a metro area’s size and its success: bigger metro equals bigger market, and a bigger market can translate to bigger payrolls. Teams in these places can shell out the big bucks for superstars, tempting them away from smaller markets, their cost spread out across more fans (read: consumers).
So how does a this relationship factor into the makings of a titletown? In our first case above, we looked at how many championships we’d expect a city to have won based on the number of seasons its teams had played. Now we’ll look at hoe many we’d expect based on its population.
We do this using a location quotient. Essentially, this compares a metro area’s titles per capita to the overall. This graph plots number of titles vs. population size, while the size of the circle represents a metro area’s TLQ, or its performance above expectation. The __brighter the circle__, the better a city is doing.
